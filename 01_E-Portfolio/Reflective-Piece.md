# Reflection on Security and Risk Management Module

This module has significantly expanded my understanding of cybersecurity by demonstrating that security is not only a technical discipline but also a strategic and multidimensional process.
Throughout the module, I developed a broader perspective on how risks should be identified, assessed, and managed within organisations while considering technical, organisational, legal, and human factors.
One of the most challenging aspects of this module was the large number of risk assessment methods, standards, and frameworks introduced throughout the units. As someone who naturally strives for perfection, I initially believed that I needed to memorise every framework and every concept before I could apply them confidently. This approach quickly became overwhelming and affected my confidence during the early stages of the module. In addition, as English is not my first language, understanding some of the academic terminology and technical vocabulary required extra time and repeated study.
As the module progressed, my perspective changed. I realised that being a competent cybersecurity professional does not depend on memorising every framework. Instead, it is more important to understand the purpose of each method, recognise when it should be applied, and know where to find reliable guidance when needed. This shift in mindset reduced unnecessary pressure and helped me focus on developing practical judgement rather than simply trying to remember information.
One of the most significant insights I gained emerged while studying quantitative risk assessment methods, particularly Monte Carlo simulation, Bayesian analysis, and Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA). Before studying these approaches, I believed that security professionals should always be able to identify a single correct solution to a problem. However, I gradually realised that cybersecurity decisions are often made under conditions of uncertainty, where complete information is rarely available. These methods demonstrated that effective risk management is not about eliminating uncertainty but about making informed, evidence-based decisions using the best information available. This fundamentally changed the way I think about security and risk management and reinforced Aven and Thekdi's (2025) view that uncertainty is an inherent part of risk and decision-making rather than something that can be completely eliminated.
Perhaps the most important realisation for me was understanding that risk assessment is the foundation of my future role as a cybersecurity professional. Before this module, I tended to associate cybersecurity primarily with implementing technical controls and defending systems against attacks. I now recognise that selecting effective security controls depends on first understanding the risks they are intended to address. If my risk assessment is incomplete or inaccurate, the mitigation strategies I recommend may fail to protect the organisation effectively. This has changed the way I think about my future professional responsibilities, as I now appreciate that sound judgement and structured risk analysis are just as important as technical knowledge when making security decisions.
Working with Moza was also a valuable learning experience. At the beginning of the project, we held an introductory voice call, during which I formed an initial impression that Moza was considerably younger than I had expected.
My initial reaction was to wonder whether our apparent age difference and different backgrounds might create challenges when working together. 
Looking back, I recognise that this assumption reflected an unconscious bias rather than any objective evidence. Although I did not allow this assumption to influence my behaviour towards my teammate, the experience reminded me that, as a cybersecurity professional, decisions about people should be based on evidence, competence and behaviour rather than first impressions. As our collaboration progressed, my initial concern quickly disappeared. Instead, the project demonstrated that successful teamwork is determined by professionalism, responsibility, mutual respect and effective communication rather than age or background.
During our initial planning meeting, we agreed to divide the work according to our strengths. Moza would assess the organisation's current situation, while I would evaluate the risks associated with digitalisation. We also agreed that I would integrate our individual contributions and prepare the final report. During our next meeting, however, Moza asked whether she could take responsibility for compiling and writing the complete report instead. I agreed immediately because I believed that achieving the best outcome for the project was more important than insisting on the original allocation of responsibilities, provided that responsibilities remained clear and the workload was shared fairly. Looking back, I recognise that effective teamwork requires flexibility and trust rather than attachment to predetermined roles. My willingness to adapt allowed the project to continue smoothly and reinforced my understanding that successful collaboration depends on supporting the team to achieve the best overall outcome, even when plans change. This experience strengthened my appreciation of professional communication, mutual respect and adaptability, qualities that I believe are essential for cybersecurity professionals working in multidisciplinary teams. This experience is consistent with Belbin's (2010) view that effective teams achieve better outcomes when members adapt their roles to meet the team's needs.
The Lecturecasts were among the most valuable learning activities because they not only introduced the key concepts for each unit but also directed my independent learning and research. Rather than relying solely on the teaching materials, I frequently revisited the Lecturecasts, consulted additional academic literature and explored practical examples to strengthen my understanding. These discussions often challenged my initial assumptions and strengthened my confidence because they confirmed when my reasoning was well founded while also revealing areas where I needed to think more critically. Reflecting on this experience, I realised that professional development in cybersecurity depends on continuous learning rather than simply acquiring knowledge during formal teaching sessions. As the threat landscape continues to evolve, maintaining professional competence requires a commitment to questioning, researching and updating knowledge throughout one's career.
The skill that improved the most during this module was my critical thinking and risk analysis ability. Rather than accepting information at face value, I learned to question assumptions, compare alternative approaches, evaluate uncertainty and justify decisions using evidence. I also became more confident in considering organisational, legal, ethical and business factors alongside technical risks when assessing cybersecurity issues. I believe these analytical and professional skills will be directly applicable throughout my future career as a cybersecurity professional, where effective decision-making often depends on balancing competing priorities under conditions of uncertainty.
Overall, this module has significantly increased my confidence in conducting risk assessments and making informed decisions under conditions of uncertainty. More importantly, it has changed the way I think about cybersecurity. I now appreciate that effective cybersecurity is not simply about implementing technical controls, but about balancing technical, organisational, legal, ethical and human considerations to support informed decision-making. Looking ahead, I intend to apply this risk-based mindset throughout my professional career by making evidence-based decisions, remaining open to continuous learning and adapting to the evolving threat landscape. This commitment to continuous learning reflects the importance of reflective practice in supporting ongoing professional development (Rolfe, Freshwater and Jasper, 2001).

Reflecting on my journey throughout this module, I believe I have developed not only stronger technical knowledge but also the professional judgement, critical thinking and collaborative skills expected of a developing cybersecurity practitioner.
